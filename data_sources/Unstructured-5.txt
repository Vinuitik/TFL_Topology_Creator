London's bus network is one of the largest and most recognisable urban transport systems in the world. The iconic red double-decker bus has become a symbol of the city itself, instantly associated with London's identity in the same way as black cabs and the Underground.

Transport for London (TfL) oversees the bus network, which covers virtually every corner of Greater London. Rather than operating the buses directly, TfL contracts private operators — companies like Arriva, Go-Ahead, Metroline, and Tower Transit — who run the routes on TfL's behalf. All buses operate under TfL branding and follow standardised fares and ticketing rules regardless of which company drives them.

The network runs around the clock. Most routes operate throughout the day and into the late evening, while a dedicated set of Night Bus routes (identified by the letter N before the route number) keep running through the small hours when the Tube and most Overground services shut down. For many Londoners who work night shifts or enjoy the city's nightlife, the Night Bus is the only practical option for getting home.

Fares are flat across the entire network — a single journey costs the same whether you are travelling two stops or crossing the whole of London. Payment is contactless only: cash has not been accepted on London buses since 2014. Passengers tap in with an Oyster card, a contactless bank card, or a contactless device such as a phone or watch. There is also a daily cap and a weekly cap, which limits the total anyone pays regardless of how many journeys they make. Travellers under the age of 11 travel free, and those aged 11 to 17 in full-time education can apply for a zip Oyster card giving free or heavily discounted travel.

Bus stops across London are colour-coded. A red stop with a white background is a request stop, where the bus only halts if a passenger signals from the kerb or rings the bell on board. A white stop with a red background is a compulsory stop, where every bus pauses regardless. Buses in Central London also operate many dedicated bus lanes, which help them move faster through congested streets, and several routes serve bus-only streets entirely closed to other traffic.

London's bus routes are numbered rather than named. Single and double-digit numbers generally serve central areas; three-digit numbers typically indicate suburban routes further from the core. Routes in the 400s and 500s often serve areas south and east of the city, and routes prefixed with letters such as E, W, or K serve specific local areas like Ealing, Walthamstow, or Kingston.

The network is also a lifeline for accessibility. All modern London buses are low-floor, with a ramp that extends to allow wheelchair users and passengers with prams to board without assistance. Each bus has a designated wheelchair space and priority seating near the front for elderly and disabled passengers. Audio and visual announcements on board call out the next stop, which helps passengers who are visually impaired or unfamiliar with the route.

In recent years, London has been gradually replacing older diesel buses with cleaner alternatives. The city now has a growing fleet of zero-emission electric buses, particularly on routes in the most polluted central zones. Hydrogen fuel-cell buses have also been trialled on certain routes. The goal is to make the entire London bus fleet zero-emission by the mid-2030s.

During major events — concerts, football matches, New Year's Eve — TfL often puts on extra services or diverts routes around road closures. Journey planning is generally done through the TfL Go app or website, which gives real-time arrival information using GPS tracking on every bus.

Complaints and feedback about specific routes or services are handled by TfL directly, and persistent issues with punctuality or reliability on a route can lead to TfL renegotiating or removing the contract from the operating company. Service quality, therefore, is commercially important to the operators as well as to passengers.
